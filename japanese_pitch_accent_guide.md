# Demystifying Japanese Pitch Accent: A Comprehensive Guide

Pitch accent is a fundamental feature of the Japanese language that governs how words are pronounced through variations in musical pitch (high versus low tones). Unlike stress-accent languages like English, where syllables are emphasized with volume and duration, Japanese utilizes a melodic framework built on **morae** (the standard units of sound duration).

This guide explores the mechanics of Japanese pitch accent, details the four major pitch patterns found in Standard Japanese (Tokyo dialect), and provides concrete examples demonstrating how pitch changes alter word meanings.

---

## 1. What is Pitch Accent?

In languages like English, we use **stress accent**. For example, in the word *present*:
* **PRE**-sent (noun: a gift)
* pre-**SENT** (verb: to show or offer)

The accented syllable is louder, longer, and slightly higher in pitch.

In Japanese, however, every syllable (or more accurately, every **mora**) has a specific relative pitch: either **High (H)** or **Low (L)**. The absolute pitch doesn't matter; what matters is the step up or step down between morae. 

### Key Concepts:
1.  **Mora (拍 - Haku):** The phonetic unit of time in Japanese. A short vowel (あ), a consonant-vowel combination (か), the nasal consonant (ん), the double-consonant marker (っ), and long vowel extensions (ー) all count as one mora.
2.  **Downstep (アクセントの滝 - Accent Waterfall):** The exact moment where the pitch abruptly drops from a High level to a Low level. Once a pitch drops within a word, it *cannot* rise again within that same word.

---

## 2. The Four Pitch Accent Patterns (Tokyo Dialect)

Standard Japanese categorizes words into four basic pitch patterns based on where the initial pitch starts and where (or if) a downstep occurs.

### Pattern Summary Table

| Pattern Type | Japanese Name | Characteristics | Mora 1 Pitch | Mora 2 Pitch | Subsequent Morae | Drop After Word? |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :---: | :---: | :--- | :---: |
| **Heiban** | 平板 (Flat) | Initial rise, then stays flat high. No downstep. | **L** | **H** | Stays **H** | No |
| **Atamadaka**| 頭高 (Head-high) | Starts high, drops immediately on Mora 2. | **H** | **L** | Stays **L** | Yes (already dropped) |
| **Nakadaka** | 中高 (Mid-high) | Rises, stays high, drops *before* the end. | **L** | **H** | Drops to **L** at downstep | Yes (already dropped) |
| **Odaka** | 尾高 (Tail-high) | Rises, stays high until the very last mora. | **L** | **H** | Stays **H** until particle | Yes (on particle) |

---

## 3. Deep Dive into the Patterns with Examples

To truly understand how these work, let's look at how specific words behave, including what happens when a grammatical particle (like the subject marker **が - ga**) is attached.

### 1. Heiban (平板 - Flat / Type 0)
* **Rule:** The pitch starts **Low** on the first mora, rises to **High** on the second mora, and remains **High** for the rest of the word *and* continues high into any attached particle.
* **Example:** **水 (みず - mizu / Water)**
    * Word alone: **L - H** (`mi-ZU`)
    * With particle: **L - H - H** (`mi-ZU-GA`)

### 2. Atamadaka (頭高 - Head-high / Type 1)
* **Rule:** The pitch starts **High** on the first mora, drops immediately to **Low** on the second mora, and stays **Low** for the rest of the word and any attached particle.
* **Example:** **心 (こころ - kokoro / Heart, Mind)**
    * Word alone: **H - L - L** (`KO-ko-ro`)
    * With particle: **H - L - L - L** (`KO-ko-ro-ga`)

### 3. Nakadaka (中高 - Mid-high / Type 2, 3, etc.)
* **Rule:** The pitch starts **Low**, rises to **High** on the second mora, stays **High**, and then drops to **Low** *before* the word ends. The drop occurs immediately after the accented mora (the downstep index).
* **Example:** **心臓 (しんぞう - shinzou / Heart organ - 4 morae)**
    * Word alone: **L - H - H - L** (`shi-N-ZO-u`)
    * With particle: **L - H - H - L - L** (`shi-N-ZO-u-ga`)

### 4. Odaka (尾高 - Tail-high)
* **Rule:** The pitch starts **Low**, rises to **High**, and stays **High** until the final mora of the word. Inside the isolated word, it looks identical to *Heiban*. However, the downstep occurs *immediately after* the word, causing an attached particle to drop to **Low**.
* **Example:** **男 (おとこ - otoko / Man)**
    * Word alone: **L - H - H** (`o-TO-KO`)
    * With particle: **L - H - H - L** (`o-TO-KO-ga`)

## 4. Minimal Pairs: The Power of Pitch Accent

The importance of pitch accent is best illustrated through **minimal pairs**—words that are spelled exactly the same in kana but have completely different meanings based entirely on their pitch contours.

### The Famous "橋 / 箸 / 端" (Hashi) Triplet

| Word (Kanji) | Meaning | Pitch Pattern | Contour (Isolated) | Contour (With が) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **箸** | Chopsticks | **Atamadaka** | **H - L** (`HA-shi`) | **H - L - L** (`HA-shi-ga`) |
| **橋** | Bridge | **Odaka** | **L - H** (`ha-SHI`) | **L - H - L** (`ha-SHI-ga`) |
| **端** | Edge / Corner | **Heiban** | **L - H** (`ha-SHI`) | **L - H - H** (`ha-SHI-GA`) |

### Other Common Minimal Pairs

* **Ame:**
    * **雨 (あめ - Rain):** **Atamadaka** → **H - L** (`A-me`)
    * **飴 (あめ - Candy):** **Heiban** → **L - H** (`a-ME`)
* **Kaki:**
    * **牡蠣 (かき - Oyster):** **Atamadaka** → **H - L** (`KA-ki`)
    * **柿 (かき - Persimmon):** **Odaka** → **L - H** (`ka-KI`) [drops on particle]
    * **垣 (かき - Fence):** **Heiban** → **L - H** (`ka-KI`) [stays high on particle]

---

## 5. Summary Tips for Learners

1.  **The First Two Morae Rule:** In Standard Japanese, the first and second morae *must* always have different pitches. If the first is High, the second must be Low. If the first is Low, the second must be High.
2.  **One Drop Max:** A word can only have a single pitch drop (downstep). Once the pitch drops, it stays low.
3.  **Listen to Particles:** When looking up words, always check how they interact with particles like ね, が, or を. That is the ultimate way to distinguish between **Heiban** (flat high) and **Odaka** (drops on particle).
